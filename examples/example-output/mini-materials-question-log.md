## Q01: What is the difference between random and layered defects?

**Short answer:** Random defects are scattered without a repeated spatial pattern. Layered defects concentrate in repeated planes or regions.

**Detailed explanation:** In this synthetic example, the total number of defects may be similar, but their positions differ. The paper's idea is that position can matter as much as amount.

**Common confusion:** "More ordered" does not always mean "perfect crystal." Partial order can still include local disorder.

**Related figure:** See Figure 1 in `mini-materials-paper-deep-reading.md`.

**Review prompt:** If two films have the same defect concentration, what extra information would you need to compare their expected conductivity?

## Q02: Why does a figure note explain the author's purpose?

**Short answer:** Because figures are part of the argument. Authors choose a figure to persuade the reader that a specific step in the logic is believable.

**Detailed explanation:** A good figure note asks not only "what is shown?" but also "why is this figure here at this point in the paper?"

**Common confusion:** A caption tells you what labels mean; a figure note should explain how the figure functions as evidence.

## Q03: What would count as mechanism evidence?

**Short answer:** Mechanism evidence links cause and effect, not just two measurements.

**Detailed explanation:** In the synthetic example, conductivity retention and defect layering are correlated. Stronger mechanism evidence might include controlled samples with equal defect concentration, transport simulations, or in situ measurements during cycling.

**Review prompt:** What result would weaken the proposed layered-defect mechanism?
